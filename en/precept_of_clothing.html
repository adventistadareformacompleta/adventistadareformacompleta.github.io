The American Costume or Bloomer

Before discussing the subject, despite the doctrine of clothing, its consequences and measures adopted by Sister White, it is necessary to invoke History to know how this model of clothing emerged.

In 1852, Elizabeth Smith Miller, an advocate and financial supporter of the feminist movement, finally decided to put a stop to her discontent over the women's clothing of her day. For her, that way of dressing was another symbol of the difference between man and woman. So she decided that she would no longer wear that dress, but "Turkish pants" – ankle-length slacks – with a short dress that ended just below her knees. While visiting her cousin, Candy Stanton, she was introduced to her friend, Amelia Bloomer. The two were in awe of Elizabeth's revolutionary style and immediately adopted the model. Amelia published the new outfit in a newspaper and from there the outfit became popularly known as "bloomer", in honor of Amelia. In a short time, other women adopted the new style as well and began to wear pants. However, not everyone approved of the new clothing proposal.

Certainly, not everyone who heard the teachings of Jesus who followed him, today is no different, for those who are reluctant who say they are disciples of Jesus, however, they do not want to follow the teachings of the doctrine of Christ, he said. "And whoever does not take up his cross and come after me cannot be my disciple" Luke 14:27 . The requirement to be a disciple of Christ is obvious, everyone has a cross to bear, the problem is who will borrow Christ's divine nature to carry the cross with love, submission and dedication, and who will feed the sinful nature to cut a piece of the cross, that is, of the doctrine, believing that with the lighter cross, you will reach your destination faster. This is exactly what Sister White saw in the church, the sisters refused to carry their cross, found the plainness of the Reformation garment burdensome and humiliating, unworthy of being worn by them, departing from the simplicity of the Gospel, finding themselves above the doctrine of Christ, as Sister White reports:

For many, dress reform is too simple and humiliating to adopt. They cannot lift the cross. God works by simple means to separate and distinguish His children from the world, but some are so far removed from the simplicity of God's work and methods that they are above it and not in it.
Ellen G. White, Testimonies for the Church 1, 2000, page 524

The inclination of most of the sisters to the pending fashions of the world was evident, she was deeply concerned about the lack of conversion, and of a divine nature in the hearts of the sisters, something had to be done to mitigate the apostasy that was creeping into the church, the In this regard Sister White reported:

Something must be done to lessen the involvement of God's people with the world. The reformation attire is simple and wholesome, yet there is a cross in it. I thank God for the cross and gladly bow to lift it. We have become so united with the world that we have lost sight of the cross and are unwilling to suffer for Christ's sake.
Ellen G. White, Testimonies for the Church 1, 2000, page 525

She lamented the sisters' refusal to carry the cross, the rays of light that the Lord had shed, with the vision of the reform garment, had the purpose of illuminating the shadow of the church and leaving it unpolluted ., however it was rejected, a fact that left Sister White saddened, but not discouraged to raise the doctrine of Christ with strong and convincing arguments, asserting that God enlightened the church with the vision of the dress of the reform, for the church to dress in a way different from the world as it should be, the imams should gladly use it, she warns that Jesus, the son of God, was mocked for defending the Father's cause, as the sisters were able to deny suffering for the Redeemer, we are not greater than our Master, if he bore the cross, suffered like a dumb sheep, we should bear our cross with pleasure. The world is in the power of Satan, therefore it will always contest and ridicule the doctrine of Christ, Sister White draws the church's attention to this particular:

We don't need to invent a cross, but if God presents it to us, we should gladly take it up. By accepting the cross, we are distinguished from the world, which does not love us and still ridicules our peculiarity. Christ was hated because He was not of the world. Can his followers expect better luck than their Master? If we do not suffer censure or disdain from the world, we may be alarmed, for it is our conformity to the world that makes us so like it, that it does not arouse its jealousy or its malice. There is no clash of character. The world despises the cross. "For the word of the cross is foolishness to those who are perishing; but to us who are being saved, it is the power of God." 1 Corinthians 1:18. "But far be it from me to glory, save in the cross of our Lord Jesus Christ, by which the world is crucified to me, and I to the world." Galatians 6:14.
Ellen G. White, Testimonies for the Church 1, 2000, page 525

Finally, the confusion was generalized, the sisters refused to wear the reform outfit, others created deformed models, with ornaments and fashions, still others understood that the top of the boot authorized the making of short dresses at the knee, while the majority of Christians devotees found the alluded dress too short, forcing the General Conference President at the time, George I. Butler , to put a stop to the chaotic situation, definitively banning the use of so-called reform attire in 1883, where he wrote:

A point was reached when it became evident that the short dress, intended to be a benefit to our people, had become a real hindrance to the cause, owing to the unreasonable guidance of many among us in this respect. Sister White ceased to speak for her, nor did she use it herself, and before long it was not generally used.
George I. Butler - The Review and Herald Supplement, August 14, 1883 / Believe His Prophets, page 265
"Why was that dress left out? And why was clothing reform no longer advocated?"
In a few words I will state the reason for this change. While many of our sisters accepted this reform on principle, others were opposed to the simple, healthy style of dress it advocated. It took a lot of effort to introduce this reform among our people. It was not enough to present before our sisters the advantages of such a dress, and convince them that they would obtain the approval of God. . . .
To those who adopted it reluctantly, out of a sense of duty, it became a heavy yoke. Still others, who were apparently the most zealous reformers, manifested a sad lack of order and neatness in their dress.
Ellen G. White, Testimonies for the Church 4, pages 635-636
The dress of reform, which was once defended, proved to be a battle at every turn. Church members, refusing to adopt this wholesome style of dress, caused dissension and discord. With some people there was no uniformity and taste in the preparation of clothing, as it was clearly presented to them. This served as a reason for gossip. The burden of defending the dress of reform was removed because what had been given as a blessing turned into a curse.
Ellen G. White, Selected Messages 3, p. 253 / Manuscript Releases, vol. 5, page 404 / Letter 19, 1897, July 4, 1897, paragraph 7
The Lord did not move any of our sisters to adopt the dress of reform. The difficulties we once had to meet must not be brought up again. There was so much resistance among our own people that it was removed from them.
Ellen G. White, Manuscript Releases, vol. 5, page 405 / Letter 19, 1897, July 4, 1897, paragraph 15
I implore our people to walk carefully and prudently before God. Follow the custom of dressing in health reform, but do not again introduce the short dress [23 cm] and pants unless you have the Lord's word for it.
Ellen G. White, Manuscript Releases, vol. 5, page 405 / Letter 19, 1897, July 4, 1897, paragraph 17
The Lord has not indicated that it is the duty of our sisters to return to dress reform.
Ellen G. White, Selected Messages 2, pg 479 / Manuscript Releases, vol. 1, page 33 / The Story of Our Health Message, page 44 / Letter 19, 1897, July 4, 1897, paragraph 8

Note how the nine 9 (nine) inch dress above the floor was called a short dress. Sister White, when she stopped wearing it, decided to wear it a little longer, but in any case, the question of the measure was withdrawn, because she was causing confusion in the church. However, Sister White recommended the use of short dresses in domestic work, according to a source of her work , she had only one of this dress, which was a little shorter than her usual dress, this does not mean that the sisters have a short dress at home and a long dress at church, see what she explained the reasons for when she wrote:

Regarding the use of a short dress, I would like to say that I only have one, which is only one finger shorter than the dresses I normally wear. I wear it occasionally. I got up early in winter, and putting on my short dress, which didn't need to be lifted by hand to keep from dragging in the snow, I took brisk walks of two to three kilometers before breakfast. I used it several times to go to the Office, when I was forced to walk through the thin, wet and muddy snow.
Ellen G. White, Testimonies for the Church 1, 2000, page 464

To clear any doubts or misinterpretations, she assured that the length should reach a little below the high leg of the female boot, with a short pipe, as well as avoiding the length that sweeps the streets, this is the recommended measure for garments. used in domestic work, such as a single dress of this size that she had, according to her writings , see:

The dress should reach just below the top of the boot, but short enough to not sweep dirt from the streets and sidewalks without having to lift it by hand. A dress even shorter than this would be appropriate, convenient, and healthy for women when doing household chores, especially for those who are obliged to perform work in the open air.
Ellen G. White, Testimonies for the Church 1, 2000, p 461 / Selected Messages 2, p 478 / How to Live p 399 / The Review and Herald Feb. 6, 1900, Art. B Paragraph 11

Once again, the impasse remains, after all, what will be the exact measurement between the boot's shaft and the length that sweeps the street? This is the question, even today, however there is no doubt that we cannot adopt "women's pants" or American attire, bloomers, balloon skirts, short or too long garments. Sister White answers all these inquiries with a THUS SAITH THE LORD , therefore, this recommendation will definitely be adopted as the dress code for this Church, all sisters should follow this pattern taught by Sister White, after all the controversies caused by the dress of the 9 (nine) inch makeover, check out Sister White's answer:

We believe it is not in accordance with our faith to dress in American dress, to wear balloon skirts, or to go to the extreme of wearing long dresses that sweep the sidewalks and streets. If women wore their dresses leaving an inch or two of space between the dirt of the streets, their dresses would be more modest, and could be kept clean much easier and longer. These dresses would conform to our faith.
Ellen G. White, Testimonies for the Church 1, 2000, p 461 / Selected Messages 2, p 478 / How to Live p 399 / The Review and Herald Feb. 6, 1900, Art. B Paragraph 11

After so much controversy and confusion over the nine-inch dress, Sister White rated the exact measurement of the women's garment, an inch or two from the floor , that's the measurement we're going to adopt. I hope and trust that all doubts have been beaten away and all sisters should look to this light, not as a cross, but as a devotion to so well representing the symbol of Christ's divine nature, dress.

If she goes out to the garden to walk or work among the flowers, to enjoy the soda in the morning air, unless she holds them with both hands, her skirts drag through the mud and dew, until they are damp and muddy. Fashion clings to this outfit, which is used in this case as a kind of mop. This is exceedingly inconvenient. But for fashion's sake it needs to be supported.
Ellen G. White, The Review and Herald April 14, 1868, Art. A Paragraph 7 / The Reform of Dress, page 4 / Believe Your Prophets, page 200

It is repugnant for a Christian to put up with all the discomfort of improper attire, out of sheer love of fashion, and to refuse to wear decent, comfortable clothing that represents Christ and his doctrine. According to White, Christian Service, page 42, reformation means reorganization, changing ideas and theories, habits and practices. We cannot give in to the sin of condescension allowing fashion to proliferate within the church, dragging its customs and idols with it, we must close the doors that Satan is pushing trying to open, after all:

There is a terrible sin upon us as a people—that we have allowed our church members to dress in ways that are inconsistent with their faith. We must rise up immediately, and close the door against the seductions of fashion. Unless we do this, our churches will become demoralized.
