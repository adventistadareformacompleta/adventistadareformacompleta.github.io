HOLY SUPPER OR COMMUNION MEETING? ACTS 20:7

The second biblical text mentioned, as proof of the change from Saturday to Sunday, asserts : "On the first day of the week, when we were gathered together to break bread, Paul, who was to set out on his journey the next day, exhorted them and the speech until midnight" Acts 20:7 . Based on this text, evangelicals and other defenders of this thesis, twist the scriptures to firm the understanding that Paul celebrated a Holy Supper on that day, declaring that Sunday is the day of worship, see, in fact, one of the texts carved at the foot of the The archaeological Bible, which forces interpretation, however, lacks solid support from Scripture:

The first day of the week was Sunday. This is the first clear reference to a worship meeting of Christians on that day, although worship on Sundays had certainly become common practice. Although some maintain that they met on Saturday afternoon, as the Jewish day began at 6:00 pm, the previous afternoon, there is no evidence that Luke used the Jewish method of computing the time when narrating events that took place in Hellenistic environments, but rather the Roman method, which counted the day from midnight to midnight. To break bread is a reference to the holy supper.
Archeological Studies Bible, p. 1810

The text of Acts 20:7, does not mention at any time that there was a change from Saturday to Sunday, as a day of observance, it simply mentions that on Sunday they were gathered together to break bread. However, we cannot ignore that in Paul's time the days were counted from sunset to sunset, according to the biblical calendar and not from midnight to midnight as the Gregorian calendar says, which did not exist at the time, so, Sunday night would be Saturday night. The question is to find out if the apostolic church celebrated Holy Communion, Sunday or on the 14th day of Abib of the Jewish month, a quarter of the thirtieth day and we know what it means to break bread, therefore, it is convenient to search the Scriptures to elucidate the controversy.

Given the insistence of Evangelicals, Catholics and Spiritualists who advocate the cause of Sunday, claiming that Acts 20:7 is overwhelming proof of the change in the day of worship, they claim that on that day Paul celebrated a Holy Supper, because Sunday is the day of worship, as seen in the text extracted from the archaeological Bible. However, they forget that the Holy Supper was instituted by Jesus on the night he was delivered. "The day has come for the Feast of Unleavened Bread, when the Passover must be celebrated" Luke 22:7 . Indeed, it is necessary to turn to the Old Testament to discover this date. "The Passover of the Lord, which begins in the evening of the fourteenth day of the first month. On the fifteenth day of that month begins the feast of the Lord, the feast of unleavened bread" Leviticus 23:5-6 .

The Holy Bible provides us with plausible data on the date of the last Passover celebrated by Jesus, the fourteenth day of the first Jewish month (Abib or Nisan) and then He instituted the Holy Supper, that is, the Holy Supper replaced Passover, on the same date. in which it was celebrated, changed the form, not the day of the celebration. If the supposed celebration of a Holy Communion on the first day of the week is evidence to support the change from Saturday to Sunday, much more consistent are the evidences that solidly support the understanding that Holy Communion was celebrated by the primitive church exactly in the dates on which Easter was celebrated, see that Jesus obeyed the law, celebrated Easter at sunset, instituting the Supper on the same date and not on Sunday. "When evening came, he went with the twelve" Mark 14:17 ."When evening came, he reclined with the twelve disciples" Matthew 26:20 . "While they were eating, Jesus took bread, and blessed it, broke it, and gave it to the disciples, saying, Take, eat, this is my body." Then he took a cup, and when he had given thanks, he gave it to the disciples. disciples, saying, Drink of it all of you; for this is my blood of the new covenant, shed for many for the forgiveness of sins" verses 26-28 . Paul confirms:"For I received from the Lord what I also delivered to you. That the Lord Jesus, on the night he was betrayed, took bread, and when he had given thanks, he broke it and said, 'This is my body, which is given for you. Do this in remembrance of me.” In the same way, after he had supped, he also took the cup, saying, This cup is the new covenant in my blood: do this, as oft as you drink it, in remembrance of me. , as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes" 1 Corinthians 11:23-26. The apostle Paul taught what he received from Jesus, on the night he was betrayed he instituted the new covenant, not with roast lamb or bitter herbs, but with his body (bread) and his blood (wine). It remains to elucidate which night Christ was betrayed, because it was on that day that Holy Communion was instituted, and it certainly was not Sunday. A careful analysis will reveal that Christ was betrayed and delivered on the night of the 13th of the first biblical month, it was a Thursday, since the scriptures tell us that "When evening came, as it was the day of preparation, this that is, the eve of the Sabbath... He, lowering the body from the cross, wrapped it in a sheet which he had purchased, and laid it in a tomb which had been hewn out of a rock" Mark 15:42;46 .

It remains proved that Jesus died on Friday, and that the first Holy Supper was celebrated and instituted by him on the eve of his death, that is, a Thursday, Jesus celebrated Passover the day before because on the 14th of Abib or Nisan he was to die just at the moment, and that the lamb was sacrificed by the Hebrews, as the typification foreshadowed. Furthermore, according to the texts presented, it can be seen that Acts 20:7 cannot be understood as the celebration of a Holy Supper by the apostle Paul, given that he said: "...Men and brethren, having done nothing against the people, or against the customs of the fathers, yet I am in prison from Jerusalem, delivered into the hands of the Romans" Acts 28:17 ."But I confess to you that I adore the God of our ancestors as a follower of the way, which they call a sect. I believe everything that agrees with the Law and what is written in the Prophets" Acts 24:14 . According to the Law, Paul certainly celebrated Holy Communion on the 14th day of the first biblical month, see that in the aforementioned text of Acts 20:7, the day of celebration of Holy Communion had already passed, surely Paul celebrated Holy Communion twelve days before the passage that mentions that he broke the bread and stretched it until midnight, as seen in the Scriptures: "We sailed from Philippi, after the Feast of Unleavened Bread, and five days later we met with others at Troas , where we stayed seven days" Acts 20:6. Note, that Paul sailed to Philippi only after celebrating the Feast of Unleavened Bread, that is, Holy Communion he sailed to Troas. In the same vein are Sister White's teachings:

In Philippi he lingered to celebrate Passover. Luke alone remained with him, and the other members of the company left for Troas to wait for him there...

Therefore, he could not celebrate the sacrament again because he had already celebrated, given that the Apostles celebrated Holy Communion exactly on the 14th of Abib, the 14th date. Therefore, it has been proven that Acts 20:7 is not about the celebration of Holy Communion, much less is authorizing the change from Saturday to Sunday as the day of worship.

In this area, he urges us to use the Patristic Church (Early) to resolve the controversy. Let's find out which day the apostles celebrated the Holy Communion. Polycarp of Smyrna was a disciple of the apostle John, in his time there was a discussion about the date of the Holy Supper, not promoted by Polycarp, but, by the bishop of Rome, I explain: this discussion became known as the "quarterdeciman controversy" the dispute took place in the second century, between the bishop of Rome and the bishops of Asia Minor, the pivot of the controversy was the date of the celebration of the Holy Supper that replaced Easter, in fact, the Christians of the East celebrated on the 14th of Nisan or Abib ( night of the full moon), the way the apostle John taught Polycarp of Smyrna and the early church, while Westerners led by the bishop of Rome waited until the Sunday after the 14th of Abib or Nisan to celebrate Holy Communion , claiming that Jesus rose on that day, the exaltation of Sunday as a day of veneration began with this idea of ​​the bishop of Rome, and not in Acts 20:7, as they would have you believe, when the book of Acts was written, the disciples celebrated the Holy Communion on the 14th of Nisan or Abib and not on the Sunday following the date of Easter. When the Bishop of Rome changed the date of Holy Communion, there were protests and disputes, the bishops of Asia under the leadership of Polycrates of Ephesus, a disciple of Polycarp, did not accept it, the Hebrew date of the Passover feast, adopted by John, was kept; for the Western Churches and some of the East, the date celebrated was different, as we have seen. Here is what Polycrates wrote, a text found in the book of Eusebius:

“We observe the exact day, without even taking it off. For in Asia great luminaries also fell into sleep [died], from which they shall awake again on the day of the Lord's return, when he will come in all his glory from heaven and will search for all the saints. Among them are Philip, one of the twelve apostles and who slept in Hierapolis, his two virgin and aged daughters, and another daughter, who lived in the Holy Spirit and who now rests in Ephesus. And, additionally, John, who was both a witness and a teacher, who lay on the Lord's bosom, and, being a priest, donned the priestly plate. He also fell asleep in Ephesus. And Polycarp, who was bishop and martyr; and Thraseas, bishop and martyr of Eumenia, who slept in Smyrna. For need I mention the bishop and martyr Sagaris, who lay in Laodicea, or the blessed Papyrus or Melito, who lived together in the Holy Spirit, who rests in Sardis, waiting for the episcopacy of heaven, when he will rise from the dead? All these observed the fourteenth day of the Jewish Passover according to the gospel, not deviating in any respect, but according to the rule of faith. And I too, Polycrates, the least of them all, do it in the tradition of my parents, some of whom I followed very closely. For seven of them were bishops and I am the eighth. And my relatives always watched the day that people separated the yeast. I, therefore, brethren, who have lived sixty-five years in the Lord, and have met brethren all over the world, and who have read all the scriptures, am not easily frightened by terrible words. For those greater than me said 'We must obey God rather than men...' I could mention bishops who were present, whom I myself summoned at their request, whose names I was to write and who would certainly be a multitude. And they, admiring my smallness, consented to this letter, knowing that I do not wear these gray hairs in vain and have always ruled my life by the Lord Jesus.
Eusebius, Ecclesiastical History

According to Tertullian, Polycarp would have been ordained bishop by the hands of the apostle John himself... Asians celebrated it on the 14th day of the Jewish month of Nisan, whatever the day of the week. Westerners in Rome, therefore, always celebrated it on Sunday, the day of the resurrection. Apostolic Fathers, 2015, ed. Paulus, pp. 131-132.

The celebration of Holy Communion on the date of the Jewish Passover was only prohibited at the Council of Antioch, in 341 AD, and it took almost 400 years before people had left this tradition of the apostles for good, under pressure from the papacy. Today, the children of Rome, the evangelicals, have also adopted the practice of the mother church of harlots and deceive the hearts of the simple, claiming that Acts 20:7 points to the celebration of Holy Communion, authorizing to change the apostolic observance of the date of the Supper and to alter the holy day from Saturday to Sunday. In view of the above, it can be seen in a surgical way that the apostles celebrated the Holy Supper on the Jewish date and not on Sunday, thus, the text of Acts 20:7 was not a Holy Supper. Therefore, we will move on to the analysis of the possibility and that it was a communion meeting, or agape party, given that, that the primitive church had as its foundation the aforementioned meetings. We will begin our study with the root of these meetings, which is rooted in the Peaceful Offerings.

The peace offering comes from the root of a word that means to complete, to suppress what is lacking, to pay a reward. It denotes a state in which misunderstandings have been cleared up and mistakes corrected, and good feelings prevail. Peace offerings were used on any occasion that appealed to gratitude, it could be a voluntary offering and also to make a vow. They were an expression, on the part of the offerer, of his peace with God and gratitude to Him for his many blessings. In choosing a peace offering, the offerer was not limited in choice. It could be a calf, a sheep, a lamb or a goat, male or female, however the sacrifice had to be spotless."When anyone offers peace offerings to the Lord, whether in fulfillment of a vow or as a freewill offering, of the herd or the flock, the animal must be without blemish to be acceptable, in it there will be no blemish" Leviticus 22:21 . It should be noted that, when the peace offering was presented as a voluntary offering, it did not have to be perfect, without defect, an ox or small cattle, long or short in limbs, could be used, which was not allowed in other offerings. "But a disproportionate bullock or lamb shalt thou offer as a freewill offering, but by vow it shall not be accepted" Leviticus 22:23 . The renowned scripture investigator asserts that:

Peace offerings were of three kinds: thank offerings, vow offerings, and freewill offerings. Of these, the offering of gratitude or praise was the most prominent. It was offered on occasions of rejoicing, of gratitude for some special deliverance, or blessing received. It was offered from a heart full of praise to God and overflowing with joy. Peace offerings were praise offerings for grace received, thanksgiving offerings for blessings enjoyed, freewill offerings, from an overflowing heart. They didn't beg any favors; they gave praise to God for what he had done, and they extolled his name for his goodness and mercy to the children of men.
Andreasen, The Ritual of the Sanctuary, page 99

Note that this offering implied a heart grateful for God's favors on behalf of the sinner, not a pleading for material prosperity. The peace offering implied communion, representative of the future Christian communion, fruit of the exchange of the sinful nature for the divine nature of Christ. The early church celebrated Agape, which had its roots in the peace offering, it was a communion feast, where the brethren ate with rejoicing, it prefigured the communion meeting, or according to the Scriptures: "they continued steadfastly in the apostles' teaching and in the fellowship, and in the breaking of bread, and in prayers" Acts 2:42. breaking bread, a doctrine that was part of the pillars of apostolic teachings and was widely used in the early church. See that the peace offerings were also meetings of communion with God and with each other. According to Andreasen:

Peace offerings were fellowship offerings. The burnt offerings were completely burned on the altar; sin offerings were either burned outside the camp or eaten by the priest, but not peace offerings, they were simply divided between God and the priest; one part, the greater part, was given to the offerer and his family. God's share was burned on the altar.
Andreasen, The Rite of the Sanctuary, page 100

The peace offering was festive, with the participation of God, the priest, the offerer with his family and guests. It was a communion feast and had to be eaten on the same day as the offering. "The flesh of the sacrifice of the praises of his peace offering shall be eaten on the day of its offering; nothing shall be left of it until the morning" Leviticus 7:15 . There was a caveat, when the peace offering was votive or voluntary, you could eat the next day as stated in the book of Leviticus: "And if the sacrifice of your offering is a vow or a voluntary offering, on the day you offer your sacrifice, if shall eat; and what remains of it shall also be eaten the next day" Leviticus 7:16 . According to Andreasen:

The whole ceremony constituted a kind of communion service, in which the priest and the people participated, with the Lord, at his table; an occasion of rejoicing, when all were united in gratitude and praise to God for his mercy.
Andreasen, The Ritual of the Sanctuary, p. 100-101.

In order to better understand the meaning of the peace offering, one can see in the Scriptures, read, when cases of disharmony, misunderstandings, rivalries were overcome and there was reconciliation, a peace offering and a banquet were offered where everyone fraternized in the presence of the Lord, priests, conflicting people, their families, neighbors and friends. The early church followed this pattern, celebrating the feast of Agape, or the feast of love, also called in Acts 20:7, the breaking of bread. The worship of the early church was communion services. The peace offering had this power, to pacify, to promote reconciliation, the divine nature reigned sovereign in the hearts of God's servants with its peculiar fruits of communion, it celebrated justification, man at peace with God. The shared meal illustrated mutual communion with God and with the brothers. In this particular, this offer was different from all the others. In Andreasen's illuminating accounts:

As previously stated, the peace offering was a communion offering in which God, the priest and the person took part. It was a common meal, taken in the temple precinct, where joy and contentment prevailed. It was not an occasion on which peace was effected, but a feast of rejoicing at the possession of it. It was usually preceded by a sin offering or a burnt offering. Atonement had been made, blood had been sprinkled, forgiveness had been granted, and justification had been secured. To celebrate this, the offerer invited his close relatives and his servants, as well as the Levites, to eat with him (Deuteronomy 12:17-18). And so the whole family gathered within the doors of the temple to celebrate in a festive way the peace that had been established between God and man, and between man and man.
Andreasen, The Rite of the Sanctuary, page 104

The focus of the peace offering was to maintain communion with God and with each other, justification by faith reconciles us to God, we have peace with God, so it was a representation of the righteousness of Christ in the hearts of believers through the peace offering, see the which asserts the following text from Andreasen's work:

Being therefore justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ. Rom. 5:1. He is our peace Ephesians. 2:14. The Israel of old were invited to celebrate their peace with God, the forgiveness of the sinner, and their being restored to divine favor. This celebration included son and daughter, servant and servant, the Levite. All sat at the Lord's table and rejoiced together in hope of the glory of God. In the same way we ought to glory in God through our Lord Jesus Christ, through whom we have now achieved reconciliation. Romans 5:2 and 11
Andreasen, The Rite of the Sanctuary, p 105

Peace offerings were replaced by the feast of Agape, or the feast of love, or by the nomenclature used in Acts 20:7, the breaking of bread. The meaning of breaking bread is the relationship between brothers. Believers' communion with God reflects the divine nature of Christ imputed in hearts (after justification by faith), in communion with one another. So they broke bread in the houses of the brothers. Only the divine nature of Christ imputed in hearts could result in Christian fellowship, see the result of the breaking of bread in the bosom of the church: "Those who believed were held together and had everything in common" Acts 2:44. Surely the carved text in Acts 20:7 is a communion meeting, or agape feast, or the breaking of bread, a meeting that was not held to indicate that Christ changed the Day of worship or the sanctity of the Sabbath to the spurious day of Sunday, but expressing communion, was a meeting celebrated in the homes of believers and not in the church as some religious with the spirit of Simon Mago want to sustain. At the foot of the Archaeological Study Bible, p. 1810, a reference note is carved explaining Acts 20:7, confirming that the early church held communion meetings, or breaking of bread, as doctrine. Including, explaining that the Corinthian believers confused the communion meeting with the Holy Supper.

“1 Corinthians 11:17... Sharing meals was a crucial part of the life of the early church. Jesus set an example by gladly welcoming all who came to the fellowship of the table. The early church continued this practice, and members often met in homes to share food (eg, Acts 2:42). Certain Jewish and Greco-Roman religious associations also gathered to participate in communal meals, and sometimes behavior in pagan environments was out of order. For Christians, however, the meal shared a powerful symbol of their love in Christ, so that it came to be called the “love feast” (Gr. agape; lit. one love) The word is used this way in the NT only in Jude 12.

Jesus also instituted the Eucharist (or Lord's Supper), and its relationship with the feast of love is difficult; Was it one or two events? The most likely answer is that in the early church no marked distinction was made between the two. The eucharist was probably celebrated in the context of an ecclesiastical meal, just as the first eucharist was celebrated in the context of the Passover Supper. It soon became evident, however, that it was unwise to combine the two: When you come together, it is not to eat the Lord's Supper, for each one eats his own supper without waiting for the others. So while one is hungry, another is drunk (1 Cor. 11:20-21). In the following centuries, the Lord's Supper was separated from the communal meal, and the love feast tradition began to disappear.
Archeological Study Bible, p. 1875
