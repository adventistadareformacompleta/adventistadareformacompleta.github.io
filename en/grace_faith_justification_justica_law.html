GRACE, FAITH, JUSTIFICATION, JUSTICE & LAW

Preface

At first, it is necessary to bring to light the effort of Satan to annul the Law, in this way, it weaves a network of unfounded theses pursuing the aforementioned objective. Focused on the same purpose, a huge majority of evangelicals, teachers of the Dispensationalist doctrine entrenched themselves in the text: "For the Law was given through Moses, but grace and truth came through Jesus Christ." John 1:17. They aim to nullify the law, exalting grace. Indeed, the law was codified by God, given to Israel through Moses, while grace represents a time lapse for Christ to sanctify the sinner by imputing his righteousness, enabling him to love and obey the law. Thus, dispensationalists make a serious mistake, converging on the collision line with Scripture, attacking the Law of God entrenched in grace.

In his view, the Law belongs to the Old Testament dispensation, abolished by Christ on the cross. Grace, in turn, belongs to the New Testament, detached from the Law. In view of the thesis raised, it is necessary to inquire, was there grace in the Old Testament? Searching the Scriptures, it is possible to glimpse the answer in the Pauline texts:"For it was not by the law that Abraham, or his seed, received the promise that he should be the heir of the world; but it was by the righteousness of faith. For if those who live by the law are heirs, faith does not has value and the promise is void. For the law produces wrath, but where there is no law there can be no transgression. Therefore the promise proceeds from faith, that it may be according to grace, in order that the promise be guaranteed to all Abraham's seed, not only to the law, but also to the faith that Abraham had. Therefore, he is the father of us all!" Romans 4:13-16. There was grace in the Old Testament, the text alluded to overturns the icy dispensationalist argument, denying the work of grace in the Old Testament. The promise proceeds from grace through faith, Read, God promised Abraham and his descendants a lapse of time, grace, for the Savior to restore the divine likeness by lending His righteousness enabling them to keep the Law. The method of salvation by grace through faith was not unique to the New Testament, as seen, the golden method prevailed in the Old Testament. Furthermore, the renowned apostle reveals Israel trying to obtain salvation through legalism based on law, works and merit. The framework of salvation rests on the promise of the Messiah to justify the sinner through faith and to supply human deficiencies by imputing his righteousness by grace, sanctifying him, both in the Old and New Testaments.

As seen, grace is not peculiar to the New Testament, in this sense Titus manifested himself: "The grace of God has appeared that saves all men." Titus 2:11 , of the Old and New Testaments, in view of the impossibility of acquiring salvation sustained by human merit, legalistic obedience to the law, or ceremonialism. Salvation is by grace through faith, anchored in the divine promise, ratified by the atoning blood of Christ our Saviour. If in the Old Testament there was no grace, salvation would be by works of the Law, tremendous absurdity confronting the Scriptures, denying the merits of the Savior.


Grace


Grace, Covenants & Law (This chapter takes Ellen White's Patriarchs and Prophets as its prism)

Translated from Hebrew. chen and in gego charis, means favor or kindness, especially not earned or earned. On the subject, Paul stands out presenting to the world the doctrine of salvation by grace: "For it is by grace you have been saved through faith, and that not of yourselves, it is the gift of God." Ephesians 2:8 . In his thesis, salvation does not result from legalistic obedience to the Law, nationality, race or color, but from divine favor freely distributed through faith: "Therefore, having been justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord. Jesus Christ, through whom we have gained full access by faith to this grace in which we now stand, and rejoice in full confidence in the glory of God." Romans 5:1-2. In the text, the celebrated apostle illustrates the blessings of the Gospel, access to grace through faith. Since sin opened an abyss separating man from God, grace, cultivated by faith, brings the sinner closer to the Most High reconciled by the righteousness of Christ, read, the atoning blood of Christ healed the wounds opened by the sin of Adam, bringing creature and Creator together, with the right to salvation through the covering of the robe of Christ's righteousness.

Finally, it is necessary to conclude, just as the law has no power to save, grace alone cannot solve the problem of sin. On the cross Christ did not eliminate the law, its validity is necessary to point out sins, helping the sinner not to continue sinning, meanwhile, it was necessary for Christ to pay the price of transgression in place of the unjust, contaminated sinner: "For the wages of sin is death , but the gift of God is eternal life through Christ Jesus our Lord!" Romans 6:23 . On the cross, Christ simply served the sentence of death by fulfilling the demands of the Law.

At first the Lord chose, exalted and blessed Israel to obey his Law, in order to reveal the plan of salvation to the inhabitants of the earth and not to become the exclusive recipients of its blessings through insane legalism.

In view of the above, it urges us to make some comments about the covenants revealed in the Scriptures. Thus, the Bible reveals with meridian clarity the existence of two laws, one immutable, eternal and the other temporary, provisional, in the same way, there are two covenants, new covenant and old covenant. As seen elsewhere, the covenant of grace was made in Eden after Adam's fall, founded on the divine promise: "I will establish enmity between thee and the woman, and between thy seed and her seed; for he shall bruise thy head, and thou thou shalt prick his heel." Genesis 3:15 . In the alluded promise, the seed of the woman, Christ, bruises the head of the serpent, Satan, achieving victory against the cunning enemy, favoring his church, extending the Plan of Salvation to all men. According to Ellen White:

To all men this covenant offered pardon, and the helping grace of God for future obedience through faith in Christ. It also promised him eternal life on condition of faithfulness to the law of God. Thus did the patriarchs receive the hope of salvation.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Patriarchs and Prophets. 9. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1989. p. 384-385

In the first signed covenant, the Lord offered to man forgiveness and helping grace, that is, Christ's promise to help the sinner imputing his righteousness through faith to obedience to the Law, with this approach the patriarchs were blessed by obeying the Lord stuck in the promise. Again, there was grace in the Old Testament.

In that vein, the aforementioned covenant was renewed with Abraham, anchored in the promise: "Through your countless descendants all the nations of the earth will be blessed, because you have obeyed me!" Genesis 22:18 . The exuberant promise pointed to Christ, this axiomatic truth was perfectly understood by Abraham: "And the Scripture, foreseeing that God would justify the non-Jews by faith, preached beforehand the good news to Abraham: Through thee shall all nations be blessed... In the same way, the promises were made to Abraham and to his descendant. Scripture does not declare: And to his descendants, as referring to many, but exclusively, "To his descendant", conveying the information that deals with one person, that is, Christ." Galatians 3:8;16 , Abraham believed the august promise, trusting in the redemptive work of the Messiah for the forgiveness of sinners. The aforesaid faith was ascribed to Abraham as righteousness.

Still in the same context, it may be said, the covenant with Abraham linked obedience to the Law sustained by the helping grace of the Messiah. The Lord appeared to Abraham and said, "When Abram was ninety-nine years old, the Lord appeared to him and said, 'I am God Almighty, walk before me and be blameless!
Behold, I establish my covenant between me and you. , and I will greatly multiply your descendants." Genesis 17:1-2 . Aware of the divine plan, Abram enjoyed the helping grace of the messiah in the shadows, lavishing confidence, expressed fidelity and kept the divine covenant: "because Abraham obeyed me, kept my ordinances, my commandments, my principles and my laws!" Genesis 26:5. In the face of trust, fidelity and obedience to his Law, the Lord confirmed a covenant with Abraham and his numerous descendants in Christ: "I will establish my covenant between me and you and your future descendants, from generation to generation, an everlasting covenant, to be the thy God, and the God thy thy race after thee." Genesis 17:7 . It should be noted that at first the aforementioned covenant was signed with Adam and renewed with Abraham, however, it required ratification: "In fact, according to the Law, almost all things are purified with blood, and without the shedding of blood there can be no acquittal. !" Hebrews 9:22 , read, remission is the act or action of forgiving, redeemed, redemptive work ratified by the precious blood of Christ once. In this sense Ellen White manifests itself:

It had existed by the promise of God since the first indication of redemption was made; he had been accepted by faith; yet when it is ratified by Christ, it is called a new covenant. The law of God was the basis of this covenant, which was simply a provision designed to bring men back into harmony with the divine will, placing them where they could obey the law of God.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Patriarchs and Prophets. 9. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1989. p. 385

It should be noted that the covenant signed with Adam, renewed with Abraham and ratified by Christ was called a new covenant, founded on faith, through the helping grace of Christ to obey the Law. In so doing, the promise of forgiveness, obedience through the Messiah's righteousness, and salvation, was an everlasting covenant, extended to Abraham and his descendants in Christ.

Once the first pact has expired, it is necessary to comment on the other pact, known as the old covenant, signed between God and Israel at Sinai, ratified by the blood of a sacrifice:

The Abrahamic covenant was ratified by the blood of Christ, and is called the second or new covenant, because the blood by which it was sealed was shed after the blood of the first covenant. That the new covenant was valid in the days of Abraham, is evident from the fact that it was then confirmed both by the promise and by the oath of God, two immutable things, in which it is impossible for God to lie. Hebrews 6:18.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Patriarchs and Prophets. 9. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1989. p. 385

Given the above, it is worth questioning, if the Abrahamic covenant contained a promise of redemption, why sign another covenant at Sinai? In response to Sister White, she exhorted:

In their captivity the people had largely lost the knowledge of God and the principles of the Abrahamic covenant. In delivering them from Egypt, God sought to reveal His power and mercy to them, that they might be led to love and trust in Him. He brought them to the Red Sea — where, pursued by the Egyptians, it seemed impossible to escape — that they might sympathize with their utter helplessness, and their need of divine help; and then wrought their deliverance. Thus they were filled with love and gratitude to God, and with confidence in his power to help them. He binds them to Himself as their Deliverer from temporal bondage.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Patriarchs and Prophets. 9. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1989. p. 386

The deliverance from Egyptian captivity was palliative, the Lord needed to free Israel from the bondage of idolatry, neglect and corruption. In Egypt, the Hebrews lost their wholesome conception of the holiness of God, of the evil sinfulness of their hearts and inability of themselves to obey the Law, compelling the Lord to impress upon their minds the dependence and need of the Saviour.

Holding up a strong arm, the Lord used Moses as a mediator. Leading the people to Sinai, he manifested his glory, codified the decalogue and delivered it to the people with a promise of great blessings on condition of obedience, verbis: "Now if you will hear my voice and obey my covenant, you will be as my personal treasure above all nations, though all the earth is my possession. You will be to me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation. These are the words that you will speak to the children of Israel." Exodus 19:5-6 . The people did not realize the impossibility of obeying the Law without the justice of the Messiah, they did not know the malignity of the heart nourished by a sinful nature. Meanwhile, believing in human conduct, they signed a covenant with God, anchored in their own righteousness, they said:"Next he read the Book of the Covenant and read it to the people; and they replied, "All that Yahweh has commanded, we will do and obey." Exodus 24:7 Leveraging a feeling of zeal, Israel witnessed the proclamation of the Law at Sinai with the manifest glory and majesty of the Most High and they feared with terror, yet after a few weeks they violated the covenant made at Sinai by prostrating themselves before the golden calf.

They could not expect the favor of God through a covenant they had broken; and now, seeing their sinful nature and need of pardon, they were led to feel that they needed the Savior revealed in the Abrahamic covenant and prefigured in the sacrificial offerings. Now, by faith and love, they have joined God as their deliverer from the bondage of sin. They were then ready to enjoy the blessings of the new covenant.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Patriarchs and Prophets. 9. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1989. p. 386-387

The new covenant transcended mere formalistic obedience, conceived of pardoning sinners and restoring the divine likeness under the guise of the Messiah's righteousness, enabling them to obey the Law.

In this measure, one asks, what are the differences between the two concertos? According to Ellen White's pen:

The conditions of the old covenant were: Obey and live – fulfilling them [statutes and precepts] a man shall live by them (Ezekiel 20:11; Leviticus 18:5); but cursed is he who does not confirm the words of this law. Deuteronomy 27:26. The new covenant was established with better promises: promises of forgiveness of sins, and of the grace of God to renew the heart, and bring it into harmony with the principles of God's law. This is the covenant that I will make with the house of Israel after those days, says the Lord: I will put My law within them, and I will write it on their hearts... For I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sins no more. sins. Jeremiah 31:33-34.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Patriarchs and Prophets. 9. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1989. p. 387

It must be concluded that the Law was not revoked, changed or transformed into a barrier, a wall of separation, on the contrary, its validity was confirmed. Changed the way to obey, removed obedience by the simple table of stone, was nailed in the heart by the Holy Spirit, read, the grace of God extended to all men, a period for the Savior to impute his righteousness in their hearts , renewing it, through faith enabled them to keep the Law out of love, in the greatness of his holiness.

Powerless in the face of the sins committed at Sinai, Israel broke the covenant, the covenant with God, they celebrated idolatrous worship worshiping the maligned golden calf, thus, it is necessary for the sinner to feel the need for the Messiah to nail the law in his heart, declining from legalism accepting the righteousness of Christ:

The same law that was engraved on tables of stone is written by the Holy Spirit on the tables of the heart. Instead of taking care to establish our own righteousness, we accept the righteousness of Christ. His blood atones for our sins. His obedience is accepted on our behalf. Then the heart renewed by the Holy Spirit will produce the fruits of the Spirit. Through the grace of Christ we will live in obedience to the law of God written in our hearts. Having the Spirit of Christ, we will walk as he walked. Through the prophet He declared of Himself: I delight to do Thy will, O my God; yes, Your law is within my heart. Psalms 40:8. And, when he was among men, he said: The Father has not left me alone, because. I always do what pleases you. St. John 8:29.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Patriarchs and Prophets. 9. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1989. p. 387)

As stated, the Law was not revoked, the same Law written by the finger of God, delivered to Israel at Sinai, is nailed by the Holy Spirit in the hearts of God's servants. Read, the Lord changed the way to obey the Law. Through Christ's helping grace, the sinner receives borrowed righteousness to obey the Law, breaking with meritorious works.

Finally, Ellen White quotes Paul's text bringing light about the relationship between faith and law in the new covenant, in his presentations the celebrated apostle says:

Being therefore justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ. Do we then make void the law through faith? Not at all, rather we lay down the law. For what the law could not do, since it was weak through the flesh, - that is, it could not justify man, because in his sinful nature he could not keep it - God, sending His Son in the likeness of sinful flesh, for sin condemned sin in the flesh, that the righteousness of the law might be fulfilled in us, who walk not after the flesh, but after the Spirit. Romans 5:1; 3:31; 8:3-4.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Patriarchs and Prophets. 9. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1989. p. 387-388

Briefly, Paul illuminated the topic under discussion, justification is effective by faith, however, faith did not nullify the Law. According to Pauline exhortation, the law cannot justify man, under the cloak of the sinful nature it was impossible for the sinner to obey the Law, needing to borrow the Savior's righteousness to sanctify himself and obey the Law out of love. Not forgetting, the function of the Law is to point out, to reveal sin, for the believer, to walk according to the Spirit and not to sin. The law proclaimed at Sinai is the same Law as the New Testament, helping Paul to know sin and not to covet. The law is immutable, it has changed the way of obeying, no longer for self-righteousness, formalism, legalism, but for the righteousness of Christ, for love and sanctification.

Furthermore, it is necessary to ask, who will be justified by faith, who obeys the Law or who ignores, transgressing? The answer is found in Scripture: "For it is not those who hear the law that are considered righteous before God, but those who keep the law will be declared righteous." Romans 2:13. Only the justified sinner, sanctified by the righteousness of Christ, is able to practice, obey the Law rooted in faith. In another turn, the mere knower, or doer of the Law sustained in mere formalism, legalism, or ceremonies, stripped of the Saviour's righteousness, will not be justified, not even by faith. Read, the sinner justified by faith borrows the righteousness of Christ meeting the requirement of the Law via sanctification, work of the Holy Spirit or imputation of Christ's righteousness. Christians cannot make the mistakes of Judaism. The Jews heard the law regularly in the Synagogues: "For from ancient times Moses is preached in every city, as he is read in the synagogue every Sabbath day." Acts 15:21, nourished hope of justification anchored in the mere possession of the Scriptures, however, the veiled knowledge extracted was insufficient to sanctify. In this way, they lost the condition of the chosen people, refusing the helping grace of the Messiah, read, they despised sanctification through the righteousness of Christ, stuck in their own righteousness, filthy rags to God, they tried the impossible, obeying the Law clothed in of a sinful nature.


Grace & Justice of Christ

At first it is necessary to ask, what is the purpose of grace? It is a lapse of time destined for human restoration, read, Christ took the sinner's place, paid the price demanded by the death sentence on the cross, eliminating condemnation, not the law, in that way, fulfilled the promise signed with Abraham of restore the divine likeness. The penitent sinner through grace will be restored to the status of Adam before the fall, renewed by the Spirit of Christ: "As for the former way of life, you were instructed to put off the old man, which is corrupted by deceitful desires, and to be renewed in the your way of thinking and to put on the new man, created to be like God in righteousness and holiness from the truth." Ephesians 4:22-24. Two natures are glimpsed in the aforementioned text, an old man dominated by the desires of the flesh, a sinful nature. A new man, clothed in the righteousness of Christ, bearing fruits of the Spirit, sanctified, enabled to love and obey the Law.

In the same vein, Ellen White manifests herself exposing the Plan of Redemption, according to the renowned writer:

In the plan of restoring to men the Divine Image, it was stipulated that the Holy Spirit should work in the human mind and be, like the presence of Christ, a molding influence on human character. By accepting the truth, men also become recipients of Christ's grace, and devote their sanctified human capacity to the work in which Christ was engaged—men become laborers together with God.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. And You Will Receive Power. 1. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1999. pg. 47

In Ellen White's view, grace is founded on the lapse of time devoted to restoring the divine image in man through faith. Surrounded by the righteousness of Christ, the sinner sanctifies himself and cooperates with the work reconciled to God.

In view of the above, it is concluded, focused on restoring the lost divine image, Christ lends his justice sanctifying the sinner:"Nevertheless, in recent times a righteousness from God has appeared, apart from the Law, but to which the Law and the Prophets testify; that is, the righteousness of God, through faith in Jesus Christ, to all who believe. For there is no distinction. For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God, being justified freely by his grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus. God offered him as a sacrifice to be a propitiation through faith through his blood, proclaiming the evidence of his righteousness. In his mercy he had left unpunished the sins he had previously committed, but at present he has demonstrated his righteousness, that he might be just and the justifier of him who puts all his faith in Jesus." Romans 3:21-26. Law, legalism, formalism or ceremonialism do not have the power to save, justify or reconcile with God. Salvation is by grace through faith bound to Christ's promises to lift up the lost sinner with his righteousness. The function of the Law is to point out sin, helping the penitent sinner, clothed in the robe of Christ's righteousness, to avoid sinning or transgressing the Law. The Plan of Redemption is based on restoring the divine image or likeness, lost by Adam, according to the Convergence theory of Irenaeus.

In fact, the Lord formulated the Plan of Redemption aimed at recovering the divine likeness by stamping the character of Christ in man, sanctifying him:

Before the world was made, it was agreed that the divinity of Christ should be wrapped in humanity.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Selected Messages, V.1. 2nd ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1985. p. 250

In this bias, grace is a lapse of time, based on the Plan of Redemption, for Christ to recover man, involving the humanity of Christians with his divinity, recovering the divine likeness, making man similar to Adam before sin:

Thus Christ gave humanity an existence out of himself. To bring humanity to Christ, to bring the fallen race to unity with divinity, such is the work of redemption. Christ took human nature in order that men might be one with him, as he is with the Father, in order that God might love man as he loves his only begotten Son, and men might be partakers of the divine nature, and be complete in it.
The Holy Spirit, who proceeds from the only begotten Son of God, unites the human instrument, body, soul, and spirit, to the perfect divine-human nature of Christ. This union is represented by the union of the vine and its branches. Finite man is joined to the manhood of Christ. Through faith human nature assimilates the nature of Christ. We are made one with God in Christ.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Selected Messages, V.1. 2nd ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1985. p. 251

By assimilating the character of Christ, the sinner is refreshed by the Holy Spirit:

The Holy Spirit is the breath of spiritual life in the soul. The communication of the Spirit is the transmission of the life of Christ. He clothes the one who receives Him with the attributes of Christ. Before the disciples could discharge their official duties in connection with the church, Christ breathed His Spirit upon them.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. The Desire of Ages, 14th ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1986. pg. 769

The sinner receives the gifts of the cross or attributes of Christ enabling him to witness by obeying the Law, sound doctrine and statutes through the regenerating grace of Christ:

He wants his servants to bear witness that through his grace men can have Christlike characters and rejoice in the assurance of his great love.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. The Desire of Ages, 14th ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1986. pg. 790

According to the text alluded to, men can recover the divine likeness by coining the character of Christ by absorbing his righteousness, that is, grace, unmerited favor.

With this approach, it is a good idea to highlight Jesus' exhortation: "So seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these things will be added to you." Matthew 6:33 . Seeking the righteousness of Christ is tantamount to restoring the divine likeness squandered by Adam. In this way, the process of justification by faith (forgiveness of past sins) is concluded, it is necessary for the Christian to seek first the righteousness of Christ to sanctify himself, in possession of the divine nature, the Lord adds the necessary gifts for spiritual development. Wrapped in the robe of Christ's righteousness, the sinner is sanctified by receiving the gifts of the cross, reflecting the character of Christ:

The Savior longs to manifest his grace and stamp his character throughout the world. This is his purchased possession, and he desires to make men free, pure, and holy.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. The Desire of Ages, 14th ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1986. pg. 791

Grace takes effect when the Savior imputes His righteousness to the sinner's heart. By coining his character, he sanctifies the sinner and enables him to obey the law out of love by adding the gifts that come from the cross. In conclusion, grace is the hand of God reaching out to Earth. Faith is the hand of man reaching out to grasp the hand of God (NEUFELD, Don F. Dicionário Bíblico Adventista do Sétimo . Grace is poured out on men, everyone has the right to extend their hand in search of justification, sanctification and salvation, however, it depends on free will to open their hearts and trust (faith) in the divine promise, Christ will impute his justice restoring the divine likeness .


what is justice

It is painful to unveil the religious life of the legalist Pharisees of the past and present, pursuing justice based on formalistic obedience to the Law, leaning on self-righteousness, human merits. Formalism, legalism and ceremonialism are not synonymous with salvation. Fanaticism, hypocrisy and self-righteousness (trust in works) are frequently seen to spread (spread) copiously inside and outside the Reform groups, under the cloak of holiness, totally alien to the truth of Christ's justice, the only guiding thread capable of to help the sinner in the path of obedience. Many of these sincere souls are induced by Satan to turn off faucets, lamps and refrigerators on Saturdays, remove sandals from their feet in services, wear robes, pray with open eyes, compare bicycle with donkey, wear white clothes, as a requirement of holiness and justice."Unless your righteousness exceeds that of the scribes and Pharisees, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven." Matthew 5:20 . The Pharisees' righteousness was based on their merits, self-righteousness, formal obedience to the Law, nullifying the grace of God: "I do not make the grace of God useless; for if righteousness could be established by the Law, then Christ would have died in vain." Galatians 2:21 . Fanatical adepts, similar to anchorite ascetics, walk the collision line with Jesus' teachings, righteousness is by grace, through justification by faith, in this sense Ellen White manifests:

It is not through painful struggles or weary toils, nor through gifts or sacrifices, that we attain justice; but it is freely given to every soul that hungers and thirsts for it. 'O all ye that are thirsty, come to the waters, and ye that have no money, come, buy, and eat... without money and without price'. 'Your righteousness ... comes from me, says the Lord.' 'And this shall be the name by which they shall name him; Lord our justice' . Isa 55:1; 54:17; Jer. 23:6.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. The Greatest Speech of Christ. 16. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 2014. pg. 18

As stated elsewhere, the function of the Law is to point out sin and not to provide justice. Christ alone can lend righteousness to the sinner: "And be found in him, not having my righteousness which is of the law, but that which is through faith in Christ, even the righteousness which is of God by faith." Philippians 3:9 . If it were possible for the sinner to obtain justice, to restore the divine likeness lost by Adam through his own effort, formal obedience to the Law, based solely on his faith and religious fanaticism, Jesus would not need to die. In fact, those who seek justice through works, human merit, have fallen from grace, for such people Jesus died in vain, it is enough to exercise human behavior to achieve the dreamed justice.

In fact, the sinner's fantasy to obtain justice by formal obedience, merit, human conduct, is nothing but satanic sophistry to keep souls in sin, far from salvation. Before sin Adam was happy, nourished by divine justice, able to love and obey the Law:

Christ alone is the 'express image' of the Father (Hebrews 1:3); but man was formed in the likeness of God. His nature was in harmony with the will of God. The mind was capable of understanding divine things. The affections were pure, the appetites and passions were under the control of reason. He was holy and happy, having the image of God, and being in perfect obedience to his will.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Patriarchs and Prophets. 9. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1989. p. 28

Sin marred the divine likeness, familiarizing Adam's heart with evil. Righteousness, the divine nature source of noble conduct, vanished, replaced by the sinful nature, bent on self-gratification. The infamous nature prevented Adam from obeying the Law as before, demanding divine intervention through grace. Who could intervene on behalf of the sinner and restore lost justice, the common thread connecting the sinner with God and perfect obedience to the law? The prophet answers surgically: "Behold, the days come, saith the Lord, that I will raise up to David a righteous branch; and he shall be king, and shall rule wisely, executing judgment and righteousness in the land.
In his days he shall be saved ." , and Israel shall dwell safely: and this is the name by which he shall be called, LORD OUR RIGHTEOUSNESS ." Jeremiah 23:5-6 . The sinner lost divine justice, only Christ, the express image of the Father, a perfect being could die on the cross paying the price of sin and restore justice, divine nature lost by Adam, enabling the sinner to love and obey again with perfection. According to Ellen White, the divine likeness, righteousness, lost by Adam will be restored by Christ:

In the beginning man was created in the likeness of God, not only in character, but in form and appearance. Sin has disfigured and almost obliterated the divine image; but Christ came to restore that which was lost.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. The Great Controversy. 30th ed. Ed. Brazilian publishing house, 1985. p. 696

As you can see, righteousness is borrowed from Jesus. When righteousness is imputed, the sinner changes his feelings, the character is ennobled, similar to the character of Christ, it is about sanctification, with righteousness recovered, man manifests a noble, righteous attitude, able to resist temptations and obey the Law by love. The sinner alone is not capable of producing or storing up righteousness. It cannot be cultivated apart from Christ.

Finally, Ellen White defines, conceptualizes justice, in her view:

Righteousness is holiness, likeness to God and 'God is love'. I John 4:16. It is conformity to the Law of God; for all thy commandments are righteousness' (Ps. 119:172) 'and the fulfillment of the law is love' (Rom.13:10). Justice is love, and love is the light and life of God. The righteousness of God is realized in Christ. We receive justice by receiving him.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. The Greatest Speech of Christ. 16. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 2014. pg. 18

Yes justice is holiness, the only way for the sinner to recover the divine likeness is to borrow justice from Jesus. In short, when God formed Adam and put his divine nature, Adam was a living soul, just, perfectly obedient, thanks to divine justice. Faith & Trust

At first, it is necessary to conceptualize faith. From the Hebrew emum; emunah; from Greek pistis. Faith develops through use. It cannot be taught, forced or marketed. The object of faith translates into trust in Christ, in divine promises and in the system of religious principles (doctrine), developing virtues, or gifts of the cross originating from Christ, reaping fruits, practices or exercise of faith, making the sinner spiritual through imputation of the divine nature.

In order to establish the concepts raised, it is urged to illustrate a parable:A renowned tightrope walker promotes an event moving from one hill to another on a steel cable a thousand meters high, pushing a wheelbarrow. Do you believe in the success of the tightrope walker? Is he able to cross the hill, a thousand meters high, pushing a Handcart? If you answer, yes he is able, have you believed, yet is this faith? If the tightrope walker turns to you and asks, well you believed, so get in the wheelbarrow and let's cross together, would you get in the wheelbarrow? If you answer, I don't have courage, it's not faith, you don't trust, although believing in the tightrope walker's success, however, if you answer, yes and get into the wheelbarrow across the abyss, it's faith, you trusted the tightrope walker's ability . In this way, the margin between believing and trusting is perfectly perceptible, faith is not just believing, but trusting.

When we speak of faith, we must bear in mind a distinction. There is a kind of belief that is entirely different from faith. The Bible says that the demons also believe and tremble; but this is not faith.
WHITE, Ellen Golden. Walk to Christ. 27. ed. Ed. Brazilian Publishing House, 1989. p. 63

Furthermore, it is possible to believe without trusting, however, never trust without believing. Faith, painstaking in trust, producing submission to the divine will renews the heart in the divine similitude.

Still in the same context, say, genuine faith wields loyalty, certainty, trust in God and fidelity to doctrine, even without seeing. It is about trust in things hoped for, proof of things invisible, faith architects the impossible, even moving mountains.

Hebrews 11:8 . "Verily, without faith it is impossible to please God; therefore, for anyone who approaches him, it is indispensable to believe that he is real and that he rewards all who consecrate themselves to him." Hebrews 11:6. Those who do not have faith do not approach God. Faith, is one of the foundations of salvation, trust in the existence of the Most High, invisible God, even without seeing him and in the fulfillment of his promises. From Hebrew, torah, "direction," instruction; dath, “regulation,” law; from the Greek nomos, rule, principle, law. In the biblical context, Law means principles or standard of conduct. The Hebrew word usually translated for law is torah, the revealed will of God, or any part of it.
